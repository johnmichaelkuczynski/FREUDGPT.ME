Socrates was executed in 399 BCE on charges of impiety (asebeia) and corrupting the youth of Athens. The historical record, primarily from Plato's dialogues and Xenophon's accounts, indicates that the charges were fundamentally political and ideological rather than based on genuine criminal conduct.

The impiety charge centered on allegations that Socrates did not believe in the gods recognized by the state and that he introduced new divinities - likely referring to his famous "daimonion" or inner voice that he claimed guided his actions. The corruption charge alleged that his philosophical method of questioning fundamental assumptions was leading young Athenians astray from traditional values and beliefs.

The deeper context involves Athens' political climate following its defeat in the Peloponnesian War and the brief reign of the Thirty Tyrants. Socrates' association with figures like Alcibiades and Critias, combined with his relentless questioning of democratic institutions and traditional authorities, made him a convenient scapegoat for Athens' troubles.

From a philosophical perspective, Socrates' execution represents the conflict between critical rationality and conventional authority - between the examined life that questions all assumptions and the unreflective acceptance of traditional beliefs and practices. His refusal to flee Athens or cease his philosophical activities demonstrates his commitment to intellectual integrity over personal survival.

The execution was thus not merely a legal proceeding but a civilizational choice between the comfort of unexamined certainties and the discomfort of rigorous inquiry.
