KEY THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS IN PART 2
==========================================

1. DEFENSE MECHANISMS: Three primary forms of defense - conversion (hysteria), transposition of affect (obsessions/phobias), and rejection of reality (psychosis)

2. ORGANIZATION OF PATHOGENIC MATERIAL: Complex stratified structure with concentric layers, logical threads, overdetermination, and infiltrative rather than foreign-body characteristics

3. SEDUCTION THEORY: Passive sexual experiences before age 8-10 as specific aetiology of hysteria, with traumatic force emerging through deferred action at puberty

4. DEFERRED ACTION (NACHTRÃ„GLICHKEIT): Memory of childhood trauma gains pathogenic power retroactively when awakened at puberty - the only instance where memory effect surpasses actual event

5. TRANSFERENCE: False connections between past pathogenic wishes and the analyst's person, requiring recognition and working through as new symptoms

6. SEXUAL AETIOLOGY: Sexual factors as the specific determinants of neurotic form, while heredity acts only as quantitative multiplier

7. THERAPEUTIC TECHNIQUE: Working from periphery to nucleus, following logical threads, making transference conscious, transforming hysterical misery into common unhappiness

8. RESISTANCE: Multiple forms including disavowal, minimization, transference resistance, and defensive reactions requiring patient dissolution

9. CLASSIFICATION: Distinction between defence hysteria, hypnoid hysteria, retention hysteria, obsessional neurosis, and hallucinatory psychosis based on defense mechanisms

10. METHODOLOGY: Rejection of degeneracy theory, insistence on psychological laws applying to neurotic thought, emphasis on sexual inquiry despite prudery

==========================================
THEORETICAL EVOLUTION VISIBLE IN PART 2
==========================================

Part 2 shows Freud's theoretical development beyond the basic trauma theory of Part 1:

- More sophisticated defense mechanism theory (conversion vs. transposition vs. rejection)
- Introduction of transference as central therapeutic phenomenon
- Detailed seduction theory as specific aetiology (later to be revised)
- Deferred action theory explaining temporal dynamics of trauma
- Structural model of pathogenic organization (stratification, overdetermination)
- Critique of hereditarian theories dominant in French psychiatry
- Emphasis on sexual aetiology as form-determining factor
- Recognition that therapy transforms rather than eliminates suffering
- Development of technique for working with resistance and organization
